Four strategies for broken ground

A working framework that links mapping, remediation, livelihoods, and narrative into one continuous design process.

Forensic mapping

Layer evidence of contamination, hydrology, ownership, and social history to understand how a site has been made and unmade over time.

Ecological remediation

Use phytoremediation, water cleansing, and soil rebuilding techniques to gradually restore damaged ecologies instead of covering them up.

Socio-economic renewal

Design productive landscapes – agriculture, aquaculture, craft, and tourism – that create long-term livelihoods rather than short-term spectacle.

Critical narrative

Use drawings, models, and stories to expose inequality, reframe responsibility, and imagine other ways of living with damaged ground.

Questions on broken ground

A short FAQ about site selection, remediation, and how research and design come together in each project.

I focus on sites marked by environmental trauma – mining, toxic industry, or unequal infrastructure – where spatial design can work alongside community organizing and policy change.

Each project starts with mapping and listening. I work with technical guidelines, local knowledge, and ecological timelines to define interventions that are realistic, phased, and maintainable.

Drawings and models are rooted in data, but they also test “what if” scenarios. Speculation helps to reveal invisible power structures and open up alternative futures.

Whenever possible, I work with residents, workers, and advocacy groups from the outset – from defining the brief to imagining uses and maintenance after construction.
