Landscape remediation

Uranium Recovery – Restoring dignity through soil

A four-phase remediation plan for Navajo uranium lands that combines phytoremediation, water purification, and new forms of agriculture to rebuild health, memory, and economic stability.

Location: Navajo Nation, US Type: Thesis studio / speculative + strategic design Year: 2024
← Back to projects

Project overview

The project begins from the long history of extraction on Navajo land: open-pit mines, contaminated soil, and fragmented communities. Rather than treating remediation as a one-time technical fix, the design proposes a staged process that rebuilds ecological function and social dignity together.

Four phases of recovery

  1. Contain & map – Cap and stabilize contaminated zones, while mapping health data, water flows, and cultural sites.
  2. Phytoremediation – Use plant communities to slowly draw heavy metals from soil and support new habitat.
  3. Water & agriculture – Re-introduce clean irrigation and community-run fields to restore local food systems.
  4. Civic and cultural spaces – Build gathering places, memorials, and paths that make the history of extraction visible while supporting everyday life.

Role & tools

I worked across research and design: collecting archival material, mapping contamination and health impacts, and translating those layers into sectional strategies, phasing diagrams, and perspective drawings. Tools included GIS, hand-drawing, Rhino, and Photoshop.
